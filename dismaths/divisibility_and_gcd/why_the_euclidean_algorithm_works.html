Why The Euclidean Algorithm Works

Let's say we are trying to find \(gcd(a, b)\) where \(a \lt b\) and let \(b = aq + r\), where \(0 \le r \lt a\). In order to show that the euclidean algorithm works, we just need to show that \(gcd(a, b) = gcd(a, r)\). Let \(gcd(a, b) = d\):

$$\begin{align} gcd(a, b) = d & \implies d|a, d|b \\& \implies d|ma + nb \end{align}$$

Let \(m = -q\) and \(n = 1\):

$$\begin{align} gcd(a, b) = d & \implies d|ma + nb \\& \implies d|-qa + b \\& \implies d|r \end{align}$$

This shows that \(gcd(a, b)|r\). Now let \(gcd(a, r) = e\):

$$\begin{align} gcd(a, r) = e & \implies e|a, e|r \\& \implies e|ma + nr \end{align}$$

Let \(m = q\) and \(n = 1\):

$$\begin{align} gcd(a, r) = e & \implies e|ma + nr \\& \implies e|qa + r \\& \implies e|b \end{align}$$

This shows that \(gcd(a, r)|b\). Since \(gcd(a, r)|b\) and \(gcd(a, b)|r\), then \(gcd(a, r)=gcd(a, b)\).
