Why The Euclidean Algorithm Works

Let's say we are trying to find \(gcd(a, b)\) where \(a \lt b\) and let \(b = aq + r\), where \(0 \le r \lt a\). In order to show that the euclidean algorithm works, we just need to show that \(gcd(a, b) = gcd(a, r)\). Let \(gcd(a, b) = d\):

$$\begin{align} gcd(a, b) = d & \implies d|a, d|b \implies d|ma + nb \\& \implies d|-qa + b \implies d|r \end{align}$$

This shows that \(d\) is a common divisor of \(a\), \(b\) and \(r\). Now let \(e\) be an integer such that \(e|a\) and \(e|r\):

$$\begin{align} e|a, e|r & \implies e|ma + nr \\& \implies e|qa + r \implies e|b \end{align}$$

This shows that \(e\) is also a common divisor of \(a\), \(b\) and \(r\).
